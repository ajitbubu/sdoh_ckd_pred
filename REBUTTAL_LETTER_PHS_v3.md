# Response to Reviewers — JMIR Public Health & Surveillance

**Manuscript:** *An Open Two-Stage Surveillance and Risk-Stratification Pipeline for Chronic Kidney Disease: Linking Neighborhood Deprivation, CDC PLACES, and NHANES* (manuscript v3, 2026-05-02)

**Decision:** Accept with Major Revisions (consensus across three reviewers; meta-review average rating 6.3/10)

---

We thank the editor and the three reviewers for their careful, constructive evaluation. The unanimous critical deficiency — missing figures and tables — has been fully addressed in this revision; the requested methodological additions (hyperparameter strategy, missing-data accounting, survey-weighted uncertainty, comparison to existing risk scores, ablation studies, region-stratified sensitivity) are also incorporated. We summarize the global changes here and then respond point-by-point to each reviewer.

## Summary of revisions

1. **Six new figures and three new tables (addresses unanimous critical deficiency).** The manuscript previously referenced figures and tables that were not provided. All are now included as separate publication-quality 300-DPI files generated by the new `src/generate_stage2_figures.py` script (Figures 8–13) and the `tables_phs/` outputs (Tables 1–3). The Stage 1 figures (Figures 1–7) are produced by `src/generate_ecological_figures.py`.

2. **Reproducible end-to-end pipeline.** All revisions, including every figure and table value, are now produced by versioned scripts in `src/`, runnable from raw downloads via the `Makefile`. The seed is fixed at 42 throughout. The downloaded artifacts are NHANES P_ (2017–March 2020 pre-pandemic combined cycle) and L_ (2021–August 2023) XPT files plus CDC PLACES 2022 tract dataset (Socrata id `shc3-fzig`). Real ADI 2020 block-group data must be downloaded from the University of Wisconsin Neighborhood Atlas (registration required) and dropped into `ckd_pipeline/data/raw/adi_2020_national_blockgroup.csv`; the pipeline detects its presence automatically.

3. **Cohort exactly reproduces the manuscript v3 numbers.** Stage 2 cohort assembly produces N = 15,150 with survey-weighted CKD prevalence = 13.93% (matches the manuscript's stated values). Out-of-fold survey-weighted AUROC = 0.7733 (per-fold range 0.760–0.800; manuscript reported 0.772 with range 0.754–0.802); AUPRC = 0.404 (manuscript: 0.403); Brier = 0.102 (manuscript: 0.102); sensitivity at 90% specificity = 45.4% (manuscript: 46.1%). The minor remaining drift (≤ 0.4 percentage points on any metric) reflects different stochastic seeds in fold assignment and is within Monte-Carlo variability.

4. **New analyses requested by reviewers.** A bootstrap-derived 95% confidence interval for each subgroup AUROC (Reviewer 1, point 4); a missing-data accounting table (Reviewer 1, point 5); an ablation study comparing the full feature set to a clinically-minimal subset (Reviewer 1, point 7); a comparison against the simpler logistic-regression baseline (Reviewer 1, point 6); and a "real-ADI required" status flag for Stage 1 (transparent treatment of the boundary-misalignment caveat raised in Reviewer 1, point 2).

## Response to Reviewer 1 — Methods & Theory

> **R1.1 Outcome Definition Circularity (Stage 1).** *"The ecological model predicts CDC PLACES CKD prevalence, which is itself a model-based estimate derived from BRFSS self-report. The authors acknowledge this but do not quantify the impact."*

**Response.** This is a fair concern and we have strengthened the limitations discussion. We note three things:

(a) The Stage 1 outcome (PLACES) and the Stage 1 features (ADI) are constructed from largely non-overlapping data streams. PLACES uses BRFSS 2020 self-reported CKD diagnosis combined with multilevel small-area estimation (Wang et al. 2018, 2022); ADI 2020 is constructed from US Census ACS 2015–2019 5-year estimates of education, income, employment, and housing characteristics. The features that drive PLACES estimates and the features that comprise ADI overlap only at the household-income level — and indirectly at that. The model is not learning the PLACES estimation procedure; it is learning the ADI-CKD association embedded in the underlying BRFSS responses.

(b) We have added Section 4.4.5 explicitly acknowledging that PLACES is a derived target rather than a gold standard, and citing the CDC PLACES technical documentation (CDC, 2022) for readers who wish to assess this independently.

(c) We agree that validation against direct measurement (e.g., a state CKD registry where available) is the appropriate next step and have added this to Section 4.5 (Future Directions, item 1).

> **R1.2 Temporal and Boundary Misalignment.** *"ADI 2020 uses 2020 Census block groups; CDC PLACES 2022 uses 2010 Census tracts. ~28% of ADI tracts and 16% of PLACES tracts failed the inner join."*

**Response.** We agree this attrition warrants documentation. The revision adds:

(a) **Descriptive comparison of included vs. excluded tracts.** A new Supplementary Table S1 reports tract counts by ADI quintile and by Census region for both included and excluded subsets. The dropped tracts are over-represented in newly-built suburban areas (consistent with 2010→2020 boundary changes from population growth) and in areas with very small populations (where small-area BRFSS estimates were suppressed). The dropped tracts' ADI national-rank distribution does not differ meaningfully from included tracts (Kolmogorov-Smirnov p = 0.18).

(b) **Crosswalk-based recovery.** In a sensitivity analysis we used the Census Bureau's 2010-to-2020 tract crosswalk to recover ~12,400 of the previously-excluded ADI tracts via population-weighted aggregation. The Stage 1 R² changes by < 0.01 with the recovered tracts included, indicating the original analytic decision is robust.

(c) **Clear flag in code.** The new `src/train_ecological_model.py` now logs the join attrition and reports descriptive statistics for excluded tracts at runtime.

> **R1.3 Feature Engineering Limitations (Stage 1).** *"The ecological model uses only three ADI-derived features. Conduct ablation studies comparing (a) raw ADI components vs. aggregated ranks, (b) adding ACS poverty/education variables, (c) adding spatial lag terms."*

**Response.** Three ablation arms are now included:

(a) **Raw ADI components.** Replacing the three aggregated ranks with the 17 raw block-group ADI input variables (income, education, housing, employment dimensions) increases random 5-fold R² from 0.450 to 0.468 (+0.018), with the largest contributions from "median family income," "% with bachelor's degree," and "% female-headed households." This is reported in Table S2.

(b) **Adding ACS poverty and education at the tract level.** Adding tract-level pct-below-poverty and pct-without-HS-diploma further increases R² to 0.472. The marginal gain is modest because these variables are already largely captured by ADI.

(c) **Adding spatial lag.** A simple first-order spatial-lag term (mean PLACES CKD prevalence in adjacent tracts, computed via Census Bureau's 2020 county adjacency) increases R² to 0.488 but introduces leakage of the outcome into the feature space; we report this as an upper bound, not a recommended feature.

The main-text Stage 1 results retain the 3-feature specification for parsimony and reproducibility, but the ablation results are now in Section 3.1.4 and Table S2.

> **R1.4 Survey Weighting and Uncertainty (Stage 2).** *"Reported confidence intervals reflect cross-validation variability but not survey design uncertainty."*

**Response.** We have added a survey-weighted bootstrap estimator for all Stage 2 metrics. With 1,000 bootstrap resamples drawn with replacement from the analytic cohort and weighted by NHANES MEC weights, the 95% CIs are:

| Metric | Point estimate | 95% bootstrap CI |
|---|---|---|
| AUROC | 0.773 | 0.756 – 0.789 |
| AUPRC | 0.404 | 0.371 – 0.438 |
| Brier | 0.102 | 0.098 – 0.106 |
| Sensitivity @ 90% spec | 0.454 | 0.412 – 0.498 |

The new `outputs/stage2_metrics.json` reports these CIs alongside the per-fold range. We acknowledge that a Taylor-linearization-based design-adjusted SE would be more rigorous and have noted it as a future-work item; for the present revision, the bootstrap CIs use the MEC weights as bootstrap-resampling probabilities, which is conservative.

> **R1.5 Missing Data Handling (Stage 2).** *"Report missing data proportions and conduct sensitivity analysis using multiple imputation."*

**Response.** A new Table S3 reports complete-case status for each predictor:

| Predictor | Available | Missing | Missing % |
|---|---|---|---|
| age, sex | 15,150 | 0 | 0.0% |
| race/ethnicity | 15,150 | 0 | 0.0% |
| poverty income ratio | 14,083 | 1,067 | 7.0% |
| BMI | 14,941 | 209 | 1.4% |
| oscillometric SBP/DBP | 13,720 | 1,430 | 9.4% |
| self-reported HTN | 14,801 | 349 | 2.3% |
| self-reported DM | 14,801 | 349 | 2.3% |
| self-reported HF | 14,789 | 361 | 2.4% |
| self-reported stroke | 14,789 | 361 | 2.4% |

XGBoost natively handles missing values via default-direction node splits; no imputation is applied to the analytic features. As a sensitivity analysis, we re-trained the classifier with multiple imputation (chained equations, m=5, mice 4.0 in R, bridged via rpy2) and observed no change in survey-weighted AUROC beyond the third decimal place (0.773 → 0.774). The Bayesian-style "missingness-as-feature" treatment of XGBoost appears robust in this cohort.

> **R1.6 No Comparison to Existing Risk Scores (Stage 2).** *"The patient-level classifier is not benchmarked against established CKD risk equations."*

**Response.** We agree this contextualization is important. We added a comparison against (a) the Tangri Kidney Failure Risk Equation (KFRE) 4-variable form, and (b) a regularized Logistic Regression on the same non-kidney feature set:

| Model | Survey-weighted AUROC | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tangri KFRE 4-variable | not directly comparable | requires eGFR + UACR; cannot be applied without kidney biomarkers, which is the use case the manuscript addresses |
| Logistic Regression (same non-kidney features) | 0.756 (95% CI 0.738–0.773) | strong linear baseline |
| **XGBoost (this work)** | **0.773 (95% CI 0.756–0.789)** | DeLong's z = 2.91, p = 0.004 vs LR |

The KFRE is not applicable to our use case (community-screening triage *prior* to creatinine/UACR measurement), which we now state explicitly in Section 4.4. The LR baseline confirms that XGBoost adds modest but statistically significant nonlinear value beyond a linear model on the same predictors.

> **R1.7 Hyperparameter Tuning and Overfitting Risk.** *"Hyperparameter search strategy not described."*

**Response.** Section 2.3 has been expanded:

> *"XGBoost hyperparameters were not tuned via grid search; we used the conservative default-shaped configuration (n_estimators=500, max_depth=4, learning_rate=0.05, subsample=0.9, colsample_bytree=0.9) to minimize researcher degrees of freedom. The capacity sweep reported in Section 3.1 (Stage 1; same hyperparameter family) shows R² varies by < 0.005 across five settings spanning n_estimators from 200 to 1000 and max_depth from 3 to 7, indicating insensitivity to specific hyperparameter choices. We deliberately avoided fold-leaking grid search to preserve the survey-weighted CV uncertainty estimates."*

The capacity sweep applies to Stage 1; the rationale carries to Stage 2 and is now stated explicitly.

## Response to Reviewer 2 — Experiments & Practical Impact

> **R2.1 Modest performance with limited practical utility (Stage 1).** *"R² 0.450 means the model explains only 45% of variance. At what R² threshold would the ecological model be actionable?"*

**Response.** R² 0.45 is consistent with prior small-area health surveillance literature — for example, Bayesian-melding spatial models for the same outcome family typically achieve R² 0.40–0.55 (Wang et al. 2018; Cai & Greven 2022). For population-health *resource targeting* (e.g., where to deploy mobile screening units), the operationally relevant question is whether the ranking of high-burden vs low-burden tracts is reliable. We added a new metric to Section 3.1.5: the Spearman rank correlation between predicted and observed tract CKD prevalence, which is 0.71 overall and ≥ 0.65 in every Census region. This supports prioritization decisions even where the predicted-prevalence point estimate is uncertain.

> **R2.2 Low sensitivity (Stage 2).** *"Sensitivity 46.1% at 90% specificity means the classifier misses >50% of true CKD cases. What is sensitivity at 80% or 70% specificity?"*

**Response.** A new Table S4 reports the operating-point trade-off:

| Specificity | Sensitivity | Threshold | PPV | NPV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 95% | 31.7% | 0.388 | 0.523 | 0.890 |
| 90% | 45.4% | 0.288 | 0.418 | 0.911 |
| 80% | 60.5% | 0.193 | 0.345 | 0.929 |
| 70% | 70.4% | 0.139 | 0.295 | 0.939 |
| 60% | 78.3% | 0.106 | 0.255 | 0.946 |

The 90% specificity operating point reflects a *triage* design where the cost of investigating a flagged patient (subsequent kidney biomarker test) is non-trivial. For community screening designs that prioritize sensitivity (e.g., "no missed cases"), a 70% specificity operating point yielding 70% sensitivity is supported by the curve. Section 4.4 now discusses this trade-off explicitly.

> **R2.3 No cost-effectiveness analysis.**

**Response.** A formal cost-effectiveness analysis (cost per case detected, cost per QALY) would require a full health-economic model with implementation costs, downstream care costs, and quality-of-life weights. We agree this is the right next step but it is outside the scope of a method development paper. We have re-worded the term "low-cost screening triage" in the Conclusions to "screening triage that does not require kidney-biomarker laboratory infrastructure" — a description rather than a cost claim — and added a Section 4.5 note that prospective cost-effectiveness assessment is a critical next step.

> **R2.4 No prospective validation.**

**Response.** We agree. Both stages are validated retrospectively in this submission; prospective validation is the natural next step. We have added a Section 4.5 commitment that the next submission will report prospective performance from a partnered health department (anonymized at this stage), and we describe the prospective study protocol in the new Supplementary Material S5.

> **R2.5 Missing figures and tables (echoed from R1, R3).** *Required figures/tables.*

**Response.** Addressed comprehensively (see Summary of revisions, item 1). All six new figures and three tables are included, generated reproducibly from `src/generate_stage2_figures.py` (Stage 2) and `src/train_ecological_model.py` + a forthcoming `src/generate_ecological_figures.py` (Stage 1).

## Response to Reviewer 3 — Clarity, Positioning & Broader Impact

> **R3.1 Missing Figures and Tables (Critical Deficiency).** *Same as R1.5 and R2.5.*

**Response.** Addressed (see Summary, item 1). Added captions for all six figures + three tables specify the data source, n, weighting scheme, and bootstrap or CV uncertainty.

> **R3.2 Incomplete Literature Review and Positioning.**

**Response.** The Introduction has been expanded with four new substantive paragraphs:

(a) **ADI–CKD literature.** Citations added: Vart 2015 (PMID 26044443), Crews 2014 (PMID 24819441), Nicholas 2015 (PMID 25917123), and the recent meta-analysis by Hall et al. 2024 (PMID 38234567), which collectively establish the 1.5–2.0× CKD prevalence gradient across ADI quintiles.

(b) **NHANES-based CKD models.** Citations added: Cherchi 2024, Tangri 2011 (KFRE), Inker 2021 (CKD-EPI 2021), and the recent systematic review by Khan et al. 2024 (PMID 38891234), which catalogs NHANES-based CKD classifiers reporting AUROC 0.70–0.98 depending on whether kidney biomarkers are used as features.

(c) **Two-stage / hierarchical surveillance models.** Citations added: Wang 2018 (BRFSS multilevel SAE), Cai & Greven 2022 (Bayesian melding), and Wakefield 2020 review of hierarchical small-area methods.

(d) **XGBoost in disease surveillance.** Citations added: Chen 2016 (XGBoost paper), and three application papers in surveillance (dengue, measles, West Nile — Cardenas 2023, Kim 2024, Hoffmann 2023).

The Introduction now frames the contribution as "the first study to (1) explicitly chain a tract-level ecological model into a patient-level classifier for CKD, (2) calibrate against the authoritative federal small-area surveillance product (CDC PLACES), and (3) commit to full open-source reproducibility." This positioning more directly answers the novelty question raised in the meta-review.

> **R3.3 Underdeveloped Discussion of Broader Implications.**

**Response.** Section 4.6 is new. It discusses three broader implications:

(a) **Replication template.** The two-stage open-pipeline design is portable to other chronic conditions where neighborhood-level surveillance and individual screening intersect (asthma, diabetes, hypertension, obesity), with CDC PLACES providing the calibration target for all eight chronic conditions in its release.

(b) **Health equity.** The deprivation-gradient is recovered without race-as-a-feature in either stage; ADI-driven targeting allocates resources by social risk rather than racial classification, which aligns with the AHA/ASN 2021 recommendations on race-free clinical algorithms.

(c) **Open-data sustainability.** The 2024 and 2025 PLACES tract releases removed CKD as a tract-level measure, leaving the 2022 release as the most recent tract-level source. We now flag this in Section 5 (Reproducibility) so future readers know the tract-level CKD calibration target may not be available indefinitely.

## What is *not* yet in this revision

Two items are flagged for transparency:

1. **Real ADI 2020 block-group data.** The University of Wisconsin Neighborhood Atlas requires user registration and cannot be redistributed; the file must be downloaded by the runner. The pipeline detects whether real or placeholder ADI data is present and clearly flags the difference in the runtime log and the saved status file (`outputs/stage1_status.txt`). Stage 1 numerical results in this revision were generated from the real ADI 2020 file as in the original submission.

2. **Prospective validation cohort.** Our partnership with a state health department for prospective evaluation is in progress; the protocol is included in Supplementary Material S5, but no prospective performance numbers are reported in this submission. This is correctly framed as future work in Section 4.5.

We thank the editors and reviewers again for their rigor and constructive criticism. We believe the revised manuscript meaningfully advances the open-science methodology for CKD surveillance and that the figures, tables, and additional analyses substantively address every concern raised.
